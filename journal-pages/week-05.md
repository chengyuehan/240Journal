## Reflective Proposal

After four weeks of learning and participating in a wide range of experiments related to data design, I found that the API experiment in Week Three and the AI capability comparison in Week Four were the types I enjoyed most. The final experiment of Week Three made me realize how compelling it can be to transform raw numbers into creative, dynamic visualizations — an experience far more meaningful than reading a chart. The AI-focused content of Week Four then confirmed the direction I want to pursue. I hope to present what lies creatively behind AI, using the concept of "making the invisible visible" to surface information that is usually hidden from the everyday user behind a simple chat interface.

My thematic focus is the AI arms race: the rapid, competitive escalation of model capabilities between different companies. Every few days, a new model is released, each claiming higher benchmark scores, stronger performance, and lower pricing. As someone who reads weekly AI news summaries, I often feel as though a century has passed. This race operates across multiple dimensions — benchmark performance, API pricing, parameter scale, release frequency, and the influence accumulated across users and markets. The social phenomenon I want to explore is the race itself: its scale, its intensity, and the power structures it reveals.

The future scenario this project imagines is one in which AI capability has become a direct symbol of geopolitical and corporate power. Whoever controls the most powerful model controls access to decision-making, economic opportunity, and influence. This trajectory resembles less a story of technological progress and more an arms race — one in which everyday users are unknowingly becoming its fuel.

The data I will use comes primarily from publicly available sources: benchmark performance scores and rankings of major models, API pricing from major companies, and other model and company information. These multi-dimensional datasets will be combined to construct a comparative picture of the race. Some of this data may be accessible through APIs that have already aggregated it.(For example [LLM Status](https://llm-stats.com/developer))

In terms of visualization, I will draw on the territorial conflict framework from Week Three — rendering major AI companies as competing powers within a dynamically shifting map of influence. At the same time, I will not limit myself to this format and will explore further innovations to accommodate different types of comparative data. To achieve this, I plan to use Vibe Coding with p5.js or another programming language to create an interactive, engaging data visualization.

This visualisation aims to move audiences beyond the abstract feeling of model comparisons, and allow them to see — through an intuitive map of competing powers — the real capabilities and positions of each model, so that they can make a choice they may never have been invited to make before. I hope this visualization will provide audiences with information that has not previously been easy to access and empower users with greater autonomy.
