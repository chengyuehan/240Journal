## Group Data Portrait

### Step 1: Collect

Based on the conversation in our group, we decided to create five questions related to our university life. After a short brainstorm, these were the five questions we came up with:

- What is your favourite course in Design?
- How many hours do you work on design each week?
- How did you come to university today?
- How long does it take you to get to university?
- how do you feel today on a scale from 1 to 10?

Following the instructions, we created a board to present all the answers from our group members. We used different coloured sticky notes to represent different people.

![alt text](../assets/week-01/dataco.png)

### Step 2: Visualise

After we collected the data, we did not realise at first that this was a design school task. At the beginning, we stayed too close to the style of charts in our approach to data visualisation. Maybe this was because I studied INFOMGT 192 last semester. Looking back at it now, I think we were not imaginative enough at first.

We each used the same coloured post-it note to answer the questions. In this way, the responses of different people could be distinguished, and all the answers from each person could still be traced.

After some discussion, we became more open-minded and started getting creative in the way we embodied our data. We used sticky notes to make models of buses and cars to represent two different ways of coming to university. For this, we created two miniature map scenes: one parking lot with parking signs, and one bus stop.

![alt text](../assets/week-01/datavi.png)

Through brainstorming, we came up with many other ways to present our data. For example, we used a range from 😊 to 😔 to show how someone felt that day. We used the number of hearts to show how much a person liked a course, and the size of the sticky notes to show the amount of time spent working on design.

### Step 3: Decode

Their creative presentation surprised and impressed me. They connected the answers to each question with lines and used symbols and icons to explain both the questions and the data. They used different coloured lines to represent each person’s information. This approach was not used in our own data visualisation, but they used it consistently throughout theirs. Unlike us, who tried to minimise the use of numbers, they still used many numbers for things like time, weight, and number of siblings. The use of numbers, together with different colours connecting the answers, made it easier for me to understand and identify each person’s responses.

![alt text](../assets/week-01/othergroup.png)

Their themes and questions focused more on personal life rather than school life, which provided information such as weight, sleep schedule, and number of siblings. Because of this, it was easier to guess who each person was.

![alt text](../assets/week-01/qa.png)

## Independent Data Portrait

### Step 1: Choose a topic

Among many options, I chose to observe and record the soft drinks I consumed with my meals each day. As a loyal soft drink drinker, I usually have one with every meal. During this week, I recorded the different soft drinks I had for lunch and dinner in order to find patterns in my preferences.

### Step 2: Collect data by hand

I chose to use a page in my notebook to take notes. Although I often forget to write things down, the good thing about this topic is that it can still be recalled later, unlike some other topics that would be lost if they were not recorded at the time.

![a](../assets/week-01/jilu.png)

I recorded all the drinks I had for lunch and dinner throughout the week. Since I do not have a fixed habit of eating lunch, many lunch records are blank.

![a](../assets/week-01/IMG_1502.jpg)

English translation of the data collected

### Step 3: Design your visualisation

![a](../assets/week-01/data.png)

After recording my drinks across the week, I converted the information into a hand-drawn visual system that shows what I drank at lunch and dinner from Saturday to Friday. I arranged the composition horizontally by day, with each day divided into two levels: the upper area represents lunch and the lower area represents dinner. This structure makes the weekly rhythm easy to follow and allows the differences between meals to appear clearly.

![a](../assets/week-01/back1.png)

Instead of writing each drink name directly in the final visualisation, I used a combination of shape and colour to build a coded visual language. Shape represents the company or brand of the drink. For example, circles stand for Coca-Cola, squares stand for Pepsi, rectangles represent Coco, stars represent Arctic Ocean, triangles represent L\&P, and the zigzag form represents alcohol.

![a](../assets/week-01/back2.png)

Colour is used separately to indicate flavour, such as dark green for green tea, beige for Yakult, orange for orange flavour, black for cola, light green for lemon, and yellow for alcohol. By combining these two visual variables, each drink can be recognised through both its brand and flavour at the same time. Under this visual language, different drinks can be quickly identified through their brands and flavours. Even non-existent drinks such as a lemon-flavoured Pepsi could still be represented using the same system.

This method helped me transform simple weekly records into a more vivid, interesting, and layered data drawing. Instead of presenting the information in a table or graph, it uses recurring shapes and colours to reveal different patterns, such as certain flavours appearing more frequently than others, or a stronger preference for a particular company’s products. This method still keeps the function of displaying information, while adding more playfulness and visual interest.

### Reflection

For this exercise, I chose to track the drinks I had for lunch and dinner over one week. I picked this because it is a very ordinary part of daily life and also something quite easy to record. It did not feel too difficult to keep track of, and I thought even a small habit like this might still show some patterns if I paid attention to it for a few days.

Collecting the data was actually not that hard. Most of the time, I did not need to keep thinking about it on purpose. Quite often, I only remembered the task after I had already finished the drink or even thrown the bottle away. I think that says a lot by itself, because it shows how automatic this habit is for me. I usually do not think much about what I drink with meals, so recording it made me notice it more than usual.

Making the visualisation was much harder than collecting the data. To be honest, I do not think I am a very creative person, so my first version still looked too much like a table or chart. It felt more like organising information than actually making a data drawing. After I looked at some of my classmates’ work and saw how creative their designs were, I decided to redo mine.

One thing I noticed from doing this was that I actually drank orange-flavoured drinks quite often. Before this exercise, I thought I bought cola more often and probably liked it more than lemon drinks like Sprite. This week’s data showed something a little different. Of course, this was only one week, so it may not fully represent my usual habits, but it still showed me something I had not really noticed before.

I also made choices about what to include in the data. I only recorded the drink company and flavour, not the full product name. I also did not record how much I drank, why I chose it, or where I drank it. Because of that, the visualisation mainly shows repeated patterns in brand and flavour. At the same time, it leaves out a lot of context, such as whether I actually wanted that drink or whether it was simply what I already had at home.

I think this relates to data humanism and *Dear Data* because it turns a very small and personal part of daily life into something worth noticing. It is not about big data or something highly scientific. Instead, it is about using data to observe everyday habits in a more personal way. This exercise also made me realise that data visualisation does not always have to look serious. It can also be creative, personal, and playful. Although I still feel limited by a more analytical way of thinking about data, I want to continue brainstorming and developing this skill in future work.
